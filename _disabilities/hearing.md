# Hearing Disabilities in Emergencies

## Types of Hearing Disabilities

**Deaf:**
- Little or no hearing
- May use sign language (ASL, LSQ)
- Rely on visual communication
- Cannot hear audio alerts

**Hard of Hearing:**
- Partial hearing loss
- May use hearing aids or cochlear implants
- Need captions and transcripts
- Struggle with unclear audio

**Deaf-Blind:**
- Both hearing and vision loss
- Use tactile communication
- Need specialized alerts
- Require assistive technology

## Emergency Impacts

### Audio-Only Alerts Fail

**The Problem:**
Emergency sirens are audio only.

Loudspeaker announcements have no visual component.

Radio alerts require hearing.

TV news audio is not captioned.

**Real-World Failure:**
A tornado siren sounds.

Someone who is Deaf does not hear it.

They do not seek shelter.

They are caught in the tornado.

### No Captions = No Information

**The Problem:**
Emergency broadcast videos lack captions.

Press conferences are not captioned in real-time.

Instructional videos have no transcripts.

**Real-World Failure:**
A mayor gives evacuation instructions on TV.

The broadcast has no captions.

Someone who is Deaf sees the mayor talking.

They do not know what is being said.

They do not evacuate.

### Phone-Based Communication Fails

**The Problem:**
Emergency hotlines are voice-only.

No TTY/TDD access provided.

No video relay service.

No text-based options.

**Real-World Failure:**
An emergency requires calling 911.

Someone who is Deaf cannot make voice calls.

They try to text 911.

The system is not set up for texts.

Help does not arrive in time.

### Public Address Systems

**The Problem:**
Evacuation instructions via loudspeaker.

Fire alarm bells with no visual alert.

Emergency vehicles with sirens but no lights.

**Real-World Failure:**
A building fire alarm sounds.

Someone who is Deaf is in the bathroom.

There is no visual fire alarm.

They do not know to evacuate.

They are trapped.

## What Communicators Must Do

### Principle 1: Provide Captions Always

**For pre-recorded videos:**
- Open captions (burned in)
- Or closed captions (CC button)
- Professionally done, not auto-generated

**For live events:**
- Real-time captioning (CART)
- At least 99% accuracy
- Synchronized with audio

**For TV broadcasts:**
- All emergency broadcasts captioned
- Scrolling text at bottom of screen
- High contrast white text on black

### Principle 2: Provide Transcripts

**For audio content:**
- Full text transcript
- Available immediately
- Downloadable
- Printable

**For videos:**
- Full transcript with timestamps
- Include speaker names
- Describe important sounds

### Principle 3: Visual Alerts

**For sirens:**
- Flashing lights
- Scrolling LED displays
- Visual alert apps

**For buildings:**
- Visual fire alarms (strobe lights)
- Visual door knock indicators
- Vibrating alerts

**For vehicles:**
- Flashing emergency lights
- Visible light bars
- LED message boards

### Principle 4: Text-Based Communication

**Provide:**
- SMS text alerts
- Email notifications
- Social media updates
- Website updates
- Mobile app notifications

**Do not rely on:**
- Phone calls only
- Audio announcements only
- Radio broadcasts only

### Principle 5: Sign Language

**For important announcements:**
- Live sign language interpreter in video frame
- Split screen: speaker + interpreter
- Professional certified interpreter
- ASL for American Sign Language
- LSQ for Quebec Sign Language (in Canada)

**Placement:**
- Interpreter visible at all times
- Good lighting on interpreter
- Plain background
- Interpreter shown full body (for context)

### Principle 6: Multiple Redundant Channels

**Never use only one channel.**

Send alerts via:
1. Text message (SMS)
2. Email
3. Mobile app notification
4. Website update
5. Social media post
6. TV broadcast with captions
7. Visual LED signs
8. Door-to-door notification

## WCAG 2.2 Requirements for Hearing

### 1.2.1 Audio-only and Video-only (Level A)
Provide text alternatives for audio-only content.

### 1.2.2 Captions (Prerecorded) (Level A)
Provide captions for all prerecorded video with audio.

### 1.2.4 Captions (Live) (Level AA)
Provide captions for live video with audio.

### 1.2.3 Audio Description or Media Alternative (Level A)
Provide text transcript or audio description for video.

### 1.2.5 Audio Description (Prerecorded) (Level AA)
Provide audio description for prerecorded video.

### 1.2.6 Sign Language (Level AAA)
Provide sign language interpretation.

### 1.2.8 Media Alternative (Level AAA)
Provide text alternative for all prerecorded media.

### 1.4.2 Audio Control (Level A)
Allow users to pause, stop, or control volume.

## Emergency Communication Checklist

- [ ] All videos have captions (accuracy 99%+)
- [ ] All audio content has transcripts
- [ ] Visual alerts accompany audio alerts
- [ ] Text message (SMS) option is available
- [ ] Email alerts are available
- [ ] Website is updated with text information
- [ ] Social media posts include text
- [ ] TTY/TDD phone access is available
- [ ] Video Relay Service (VRS) is supported
- [ ] Sign language interpretation is provided for major announcements
- [ ] Multiple communication channels are used
- [ ] Emergency hotline accepts text/SMS

## Technology Solutions

### Text-to-911
Enable texting to 911 emergency services.

**Requirements:**
- Available 24/7
- Response time under 60 seconds
- Dispatchers trained in text communication
- Documented as available option

### Emergency Alert Apps
Mobile apps with push notifications.

**Features:**
- Text-based alerts
- Vibration alerts
- Visual indicators
- Location-based
- Offline capable

### Visual Alert Systems
Smart home integration.

**Examples:**
- Flash lights when siren sounds
- Vibrate smart watch
- Visual doorbell
- Bed shaker for fire alarm

### Captioning Services
Real-time captioning for live events.

**Services:**
- CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation)
- Live caption apps
- Automated captions as backup only

## Case Study: Success

### Alberta Emergency Alert System (Canada)

**What they did:**
- Multi-channel distribution (TV, radio, web, SMS)
- All TV alerts include captions and text crawler
- Mobile app with push notifications
- Website updates simultaneously
- Social media posts with full text
- Email alert system
- Sign language interpreter for major events

**Result:**
98% of Deaf and hard of hearing residents receive emergency alerts.

## Case Study: Failure

### 2011 Tōhoku Earthquake and Tsunami

**What went wrong:**
- Warning system primarily audio sirens
- TV alerts not captioned quickly enough
- No text-based alert system
- No visual alerts in public spaces

**Result:**
Deaf community had higher mortality rate.

Many did not receive warnings in time.

## Communication Protocol

### Critical Alerts (Life-Threatening)

1. Send via all channels simultaneously
2. Include captions on all video
3. Use simple, direct language
4. Repeat every 30 minutes
5. Update with new information

### Important Alerts (Safety Issues)

1. Send via multiple channels
2. Include captions
3. Provide transcript
4. Update every 2 hours

### Information Alerts (Awareness)

1. Post on website
2. Post on social media
3. Include captions if video
4. Update daily

## Testing

### Before Emergency Season

- [ ] Test text-to-911
- [ ] Verify caption services are contracted
- [ ] Check visual alert systems work
- [ ] Train staff on Deaf communication
- [ ] Review communication protocols
- [ ] Test all alert channels

### During an Emergency

- [ ] Verify messages sent via all channels
- [ ] Check captions are appearing
- [ ] Monitor for delivery failures
- [ ] Respond to text inquiries
- [ ] Update all platforms simultaneously

## The Bottom Line

Hearing accessibility is survival accessibility.

If someone cannot hear your alert, they cannot act.

Provide captions always.

Provide transcripts always.

Use visual alerts.

Offer text-based communication.

Use multiple channels.

Never rely on audio alone.

**Clear hearing access saves lives.**
